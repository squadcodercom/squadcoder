# US Citizens on the Vatik Track: The Dual-Tax Trap

## The trap in one paragraph

The Israeli section 14 exemption is an ISRAELI-side exemption. It zeroes out Israeli tax on foreign income. It does NOT zero out US tax. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. The US-Israel Income Tax Treaty 1995 (Article 6 paragraph 3) contains a "saving clause" that lets the United States tax its citizens as if the treaty had not come into effect. So a vatik returnee who is also a US citizen pays full US tax on the same income that Israel exempts. And because no Israeli tax was paid, there is no foreign tax credit (FTC) to offset the US bill. This is the single most common surprise for returnees from California, New York, or any other US location.

## Why the FTC does not save you

The standard logic is: US citizen earns foreign income, foreign country taxes it, US allows a foreign tax credit dollar-for-dollar to avoid double taxation. The FTC is only as large as the foreign tax actually paid. If Israel exempts the income under section 14, you paid zero Israeli tax on it. Your US FTC against that income is also zero. So you pay the full US rate.

For US citizens, the section 14 exemption effectively delivers no net benefit on foreign passive income that the US would tax anyway (interest, dividends, capital gains, US-source income channeled through Israel). The benefit is partially preserved only on income that the US itself would exempt or treat at a lower rate (and there are very few such items).

## What the treaty saving clause says

Article 6(3) of the US-Israel Income Tax Treaty 1995:

> "a Contracting State may tax its residents and its citizens as if this Convention had not come into effect"

This means: even though the treaty exists, the US can tax US citizens living in Israel as if there were no treaty at all. Israel cannot use treaty arguments to reduce the US tax bill of a dual citizen.

## What to do (planning)

1. **Identify US-person status early.** Includes US citizens AND green-card holders. Renouncing US citizenship has its own tax and immigration consequences (exit tax under IRC 877A); decide separately.
2. **Engage a US-Israel cross-border CPA** before any major financial events (large RSU vest, sale of US assets, IRA/401(k) conversion, US business income). The dual-tax planning is delicate and the standard Israeli toshav-chozer-vatik playbook does not account for the US side.
3. **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) and FTC stacking**: there are limited US-side tools (FEIE under IRC 911, FTC under IRC 901, foreign housing exclusion). They do not always combine with section 14 the way naive planning assumes.
4. **Report obligations on the US side stay alive throughout the 10-year window**: Form 1040 with Schedule B and Form 8938 (FATCA), FBAR (FinCEN 114) for foreign accounts, Form 5471 for controlled foreign corporations, Form 3520 for foreign trusts and large foreign gifts. These do not go away because Israel exempts the underlying income.

## Citizens of other worldwide-tax countries

The US is the most common example, but the trap applies to ANY citizenship that taxes worldwide income regardless of residence. As of 2026, that short list also includes Eritrea and a handful of edge cases. Most other countries (Canada, UK, Germany, France, Australia) tax based on tax residency, so once you become an Israeli tax resident and break residency in those countries, their tax stops.
