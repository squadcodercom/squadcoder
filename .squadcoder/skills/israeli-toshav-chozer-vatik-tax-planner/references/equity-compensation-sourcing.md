# Equity Compensation (RSUs, Options) Sourcing for Returning Residents

## The trap

Many returnees assume "the grant was made before I moved back, so the RSU is foreign-source and exempt under section 14." This is wrong. The Israel Tax Authority sources equity compensation by the location where the work was performed during the VESTING period, not by the grant date.

If a US employer grants RSUs in 2024 that vest 25% per year through 2028, and the employee becomes an Israeli tax resident on 1 January 2026, then:

- The portion that vested before 1.1.2026 is foreign-source (work performed abroad).
- The portion that vests after 1.1.2026 is sourced by where the employee worked during the vesting period. If they work in Israel, that portion is Israeli-source even though the grant pre-dates the move.

## The principle

S. Horowitz commentary on equity compensation for new Israeli residents:

> "The place of production of income in this case will be determined according to the place where the work was performed during the vesting period of the options (i.e., part of the income may be classified as 'Israeli' and the balance as income produced abroad)."

The Israeli portion is taxable at full Israeli rates and rules (section 102 trustee tracks have their own complications). The foreign portion may be exempt under section 14 for vatik returnees.

## Practical consequences for vatik returnees

1. **Pre-move grant, post-move vest, work performed in Israel**: largely Israeli-source. Section 14 exemption does NOT shield this. Plan as if it were a regular Israeli salary RSU.
2. **Pre-move grant, post-move vest, work performed remotely for a US employer from Israel**: still mostly Israeli-source. The payer being in the US does not change the work-performed test.
3. **Fully-vested-before-move equity, sold after move**: sale is a capital gain. Foreign-source if the underlying shares are foreign. Under vatik this can be exempt for 10 years under section 14.
4. **Section 102 trustee equity** (Israeli-employer grant): separate regime entirely. Outside scope of section 14 in most cases.

## Why this matters in this skill

The original Example 1 in this skill incorrectly said "RSU vesting in 2028 (granted before the move): foreign capital gain, exempt." That treated RSU income as a capital gain (it is ordinary employment income for the vesting portion) AND ignored the vesting-period sourcing rule. The corrected example splits the RSU into the portion vesting on Israel-located work (taxable) and the portion vesting on US-located work pre-move (potentially exempt).

## What to do

Engage a CPA or attorney who specializes in cross-border equity compensation BEFORE any vest. The sourcing analysis depends on documentary evidence of where the employee worked each month of the vesting period. Calendars, travel records, employer attestations matter.
