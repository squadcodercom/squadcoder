# Section 14 of the Income Tax Ordinance, Mechanics

Section 14 of the Israeli Income Tax Ordinance (פקודת מס הכנסה [נוסח חדש]) is the statutory home of the 10-year foreign-income exemption for olim chadashim and toshav chozer vatik. The definitions live in section 1; the substantive exemption lives in section 14.

## Who qualifies as toshav chozer vatik

| Test | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Years as foreign tax resident, immediately before return | At least 10 consecutive years |
| Status during those years | Foreign tax resident under the Ordinance (center-of-life test outside Israel) |
| Continuity | The 10 years must be CONTINUOUS foreign residency. A center-of-life break (e.g. a long sabbatical back in Israel) within the 10-year window can disqualify vatik status and drop the returnee to regular track or no track. |

The 10-year clock for the foreign-resident period is separate from the 10-year exemption clock for after the return. Do not confuse them.

### Center-of-life break during the 10-year qualifying window

BSH CPA emphasizes the continuity requirement: a returnee must have severed Israeli tax residency and remained a foreign tax resident for at least 10 consecutive years (10 שנים רצופות). A multi-month return to Israel during the window, where center-of-life shifts back (family, primary home, job), can break the chain even if the returnee then leaves again. Examples:

- Years 1-4 abroad, year 5 a 13-month sabbatical in Israel with family enrolled in Israeli schools, years 6-11 abroad. Vatik status likely DENIED; the year-5 residency reset the clock. The returnee qualifies under vatik only after another 10 continuous years abroad after the sabbatical.
- Years 1-10 abroad, brief 2-week visits to Israel for family events. Visits this short do not shift center of life and generally do NOT break continuity. Day-count and center-of-life tests both stay outside.

Borderline cases (3 to 12 month stays, family in Israel part of the time, etc.) require a CPA review with documentary evidence. The Tax Authority can audit the chain on assessment.

## What is exempt (vatik)

For 10 years starting on the date the individual becomes an Israeli tax resident again:

1. **Foreign active income** (employment, business, self-employment) sourced and performed outside Israel.
2. **Foreign passive income** (dividends, interest, rentals, royalties, pension) from assets and arrangements outside Israel.
3. **Capital gains** on the sale of foreign assets, including assets acquired before the return.
4. **Foreign pensions** including from foreign social security systems.

## What is NOT exempt

1. **Israeli-source income** earned during the 10-year window. Salary from an Israeli employer, business income from Israeli customers, rent from Israeli property, dividends from Israeli companies, interest from Israeli bonds, gains from Israeli securities (TASE) are all taxed at regular Israeli rates.
2. **Mixed-source income** where part of the activity was performed in Israel. The exemption is pro-rated to the foreign-source portion.
3. **Equity compensation sourced by vesting** (see `equity-compensation-sourcing.md`). Pre-move grant + post-move vest + work performed in Israel = largely Israeli-source, regardless of grant date.
4. **Income through Israeli-resident intermediaries** that the Tax Authority can re-characterize as Israeli-source.
5. **Certain CFC (Controlled Foreign Company) flows**: dividends from a foreign company the returnee controls (>25%) can be re-characterized. Trust distributions where the returnee is settlor or beneficiary can also be re-characterized. These are technical and require a CPA opinion.

### Israeli-source income table (taxable throughout the 10-year window)

| Stream | Treatment under vatik |
|---|---|
| TASE securities trading and Israeli mutual funds | Taxable at standard Israeli capital gains rates (25% nominal as of 2026) |
| Dividends from Israeli companies | Taxable at 25% (or higher for substantial holders) |
| Rental income from Israeli real estate | Taxable under the residential exemption track or 10% track or marginal track per Israeli rules |
| Salary from Israeli employer (work performed in Israel) | Taxable at marginal rates, subject to the new 2026 labor-income caps (see below) |
| Interest from Israeli bank deposits and Israeli bonds | Taxable at standard rates |
| Business income from Israeli customers | Taxable at marginal/corporate rates |

A returnee who opens a Bank Leumi or IBI brokerage and starts trading TASE securities will pay normal Israeli capital gains tax on those trades for every year of the vatik window. Section 14 does NOT shield Israeli-source investment income.

## The 10-year clock

The exemption clock starts on the day the individual becomes an Israeli tax resident under the center-of-life test, NOT on the day of physical landing at Ben Gurion. These two dates can be months apart in either direction:

- Returnee lands in Tel Aviv on 1 March 2026 but keeps their US apartment and family in the US until 1 September 2026. Center-of-life shifted on 1 September. The exemption runs from 1 September 2026 to 31 August 2036.
- Returnee lands in Tel Aviv on 1 September 2026 but had already established Israeli center-of-life on 1 March 2026 because their family moved earlier and they were already commuting. Exemption runs from 1 March 2026.

Choosing the exact tax-residency start date is a planning decision. A CPA can help structure events around the desired date.

## Section 14(b), Year of Acclimation (Shnat Hashtagrut)

A separate election under section 14(b) lets a returnee defer being treated as an Israeli tax resident for one additional year (the "year of acclimation"). This is sometimes used to delay the start of the 10-year exemption clock so it lines up with the start of foreign income flows.

**Critical: the election must be filed in writing within 90 days of arrival in Israel** (90 ימים ממועד הגעתו לישראל), using Form 1130, the official year-of-acclimation election notice. This is the rule from ITA Circular 1/2011 (חוזר מס הכנסה 1/2011). The election is irrevocable for that year.

If the 90-day window is missed, the deferral is UNAVAILABLE; the returnee is treated as having begun Israeli tax residency on the actual residency date with no acclimation buffer. The Tax Authority director has discretion to accept a late filing where there is evidence the individual lacked clear intent to establish permanent Israeli residency at the time of arrival, but this is a discretionary remedy and should not be relied on.

A planning consequence of the 90-day rule: time-sensitive returnees should engage a CPA BEFORE landing in Israel or within the first 30 to 60 days, not at the end of the first tax year.

## How the exemption is claimed

There is no single "election form" for the section 14 exemption itself. The exemption is claimed implicitly by:

1. Filing an annual return (Form 1301) as an Israeli resident.
2. Reporting foreign income on Schedule D-1 (Nispach D-1) at the exempt rate (after 1.1.2026 reporting change, see `2026-reporting-change.md`).
3. Optionally holding a Returning Resident Certificate (Te'udat Toshav Chozer) from the Ministry of Aliyah and Integration. Note: this certificate is an administrative document useful for absorption services. It is NOT binding on the Tax Authority; section 14 status is determined independently by Mas Hachnasa under the Ordinance. BSH CPA writes: "תעודת תושב חוזר ותיק המוענקת ע"י משרד הקליטה אינה מקנה הטבות מס מכוח מעמד תושב חוזר כהגדרתו בפקודת מס הכנסה ואינה מחייבת את רשות המסים בעניין תושבות לצורכי מס."
4. For section 14(b) year-of-acclimation, a separate Form 1130 filed within 90 days of arrival.

For bank-level treatment of foreign-currency deposits, Form 2409 is commonly requested by Israeli banks within 14 days of any incoming foreign transfer. The 14-day window is a bank convention, not a statute, and varies in practice. Check with the specific bank.

## Capital losses during the exempt window

This is a frequent surprise. Capital losses incurred on foreign assets during the 10-year exempt window are NOT carried forward against later gains. The rule comes from the matching principle (עקרון ההקבלה) under section 92: a loss is offsettable only if a gain from the same transaction would have been Israeli-taxable. Since the exemption made any gain tax-exempt, the corresponding loss is symmetrically non-deductible.

Practical consequence for crypto and stock-heavy returnees in 2026: if you sell a foreign holding at a $50,000 loss during your vatik window, you cannot use that loss to offset a $200,000 gain in year 11 (post-window). The loss simply disappears. Time-sell carefully.

ITA Circular 10/2025 (חוזר מס הכנסה 10/2025), published November 2025, clarifies the section 92 matching principle in detail and confirms this treatment for olim and toshav chozer vatik exemption periods.

## 2026 Israeli-source labor income caps

Separate from section 14, the 2026 חוק עידוד עלייה לישראל וחזרה אליה (הוראת שעה), התשפ"ו-2026 created an additional exemption on ISRAELI-source labor income for olim and vatik returnees who arrive in the window 5.11.2025 to 31.12.2026. The annual caps are:

- 2026: up to 600,000 NIS
- 2027: up to 1,000,000 NIS
- 2028: up to 1,000,000 NIS
- 2029: up to 350,000 NIS
- 2030: up to 150,000 NIS

This is the single biggest planning lever for a 2026 returnee with a high Israeli salary. The exemption is on top of the section 14 exemption on foreign income; together they shield substantial total income in the early years.
